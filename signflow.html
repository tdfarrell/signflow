ASL Grammar
ASL is not English with hands — it's a complete, distinct language

ASL Does Not Follow English Word Order

English uses Subject-Verb-Object (SVO). ASL primarily uses Topic-Comment and often Object-Subject-Verb (OSV) structures. This is the most critical and most overlooked aspect of achieving real fluency.

English (SVO)
"I eat pizza"
Subject → Verb → Object
ASL (Topic-Comment)
PIZZA, ME EAT
Topic established first. Eyebrows raise on PIZZA. Comment follows.

The comma represents a non-manual marker — a slight pause and eyebrow raise that marks the topic boundary. Grammar expressed on the face, not the hands.

Time Signs Come First

English
"I went to the store yesterday"
Time marker at the end
ASL
YESTERDAY, STORE ME GO
Time first. Sets temporal context for everything that follows.

Topic-Comment Structure

The topic is what you're talking about. The comment is what you say about it. ASL establishes topic first with raised eyebrows, then delivers the comment.

Example: "That movie was boring"
MOVIETOPIC ↗
→
BORINGCOMMENT

Eyebrows raise on MOVIE. Neutral (or furrowed) for BORING.

Example: "Do you have a dog?"
DOGTOPIC ↗
→
YOU HAVE?

Facial Expressions Are Grammar — Not Emotion

This is the biggest misconception hearing learners have. In ASL, facial expressions are grammatical markers. They indicate questions, conditionals, topics, and intensity. Signing without them is like writing without punctuation.

🤨
Furrowed Brows
Wh- questions: WHO, WHAT, WHERE, WHY, HOW
🙂
Raised Brows
Yes/No questions and topic markers
😤
Puffed Cheeks
Intensity modifiers — "very small" vs "enormous"

Quick Check

You want to ask "Where do you live?" What facial expression do you hold throughout?

Space Is Grammar

ASL uses 3D space as grammatical structure. Nouns are assigned locations in signing space, and pointing back to those locations replaces pronouns. This is spatial grammar.

Step by step:
1. Establish JOHN
Sign JOHN → point right. John lives at "right" in space.
2. Establish MARY
Sign MARY → point left. Mary lives at "left" in space.
3. Use space as pronouns
Point right = him (John). Point left = her (Mary).
4. Direct verbs through space
Move verb right→left = "John gave Mary" — no names needed again.

Quick Check

After establishing SARA at a location to your right, how do you sign "she" when referring to Sara?

Negation in ASL

ASL negates with a negative sign (NOT, DON'T, CAN'T) or — more naturally — a head shake throughout the phrase. The head shake is a non-manual marker that negates everything it accompanies.

English
"I don't like coffee"
Inserts "don't" between subject and verb
ASL method 1
COFFEE, ME LIKE NOT
NOT comes after the verb. Topic-comment maintained.
ASL method 2 (more natural)
COFFEE, ME LIKE 〰️
Head shake accompanies ME LIKE. No NOT sign needed. Fluent signers use this.
Incorporated negation
DON'T-WANT, DON'T-KNOW, CAN'T
These are single signs with negation built in — more efficient than two separate signs.
